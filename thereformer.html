The Reformer


You hurried us past the scrum around a white sheet
on the sidewalk. The next time we didn’t stop to gawk
at the dredged cove. I know it’s bitter of me to think

of these deaths when I address you but as you said
you’ve just got to get rid of stuff, it's so liberating.
When you wouldn't come to the march I moved alone

through a long knot of living people, then returned
to the house to help clean before the sale. Help me
stop connecting these times you wouldn’t look

into a narrative I can make use of. Help me act
more like marble— marmoreal, the word is,
which has in it milk animals and death.

=

In my bag I carry your kimono— radically
appropriated— so it might get worn,

so years of dust might get punched out of it
by my radically inappropriate form.

=

Around us goodbyes are being said, drawn-out hugs
and crying jags. Goodbyes with several acts. Your eyes pace
my outline until you pull goggles over them, then a mask

against the stale air that would breach mouth and nose.
I refuse to think it is only a scarf that obscures your face.
I consider emulating your stride through the sliding doors

or trying to, jogging to keep up as you buzz through
the loose groups of farewellers out to the concourse
straight-backed as every day after we left for school

when you strapped your wrists and ankles to weights
and let the machine do the lengthening.
I don’t know where you— my mother— live.
