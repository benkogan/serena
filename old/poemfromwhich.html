Poem from which I removed the pants he made—
a pair of trousers patterned with grapes— and filled
the space where they were with my own images


An island in the shape of a soft-edged star, a parade
of women wearing only hard hats, some muddy sneakers—
which of these is the answer? He would turn me by

my shoulders until I saw whatever hemmed or studded
thing. Then I’d zip myself into it. A lake, a runway, a pair
of banged-up feet— I click through and arrive at the top again.

Once, in September, we passed through concentric orbits.
He wore a decadent necklace. I didn’t like to see him sew
his label into the linings. I wanted both sides of the fabric

bare, for him to leave unsigned his linen sculptures.
His mind was not his hands, which were not the stitches,
but the distance was shrinking. So was the range of what

we could talk about. A foggy park, a prickly pop star,
a night so dark I feared there might be bears. I learned
something from watching, from being measured:

that my gaze is too a knife, as cold and sharp
as anyone's. The problem was not the label
but the maldistribution of threads that connected us

bunched up as they are at the beginning. We canoed,
I waited for a long time to see him, we walked together
from a dark wood house. When we spoke we both spoke.
