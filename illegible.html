The Illegible Heirloom

When the journal arrives after the funeral,
perfumed with incense as if itself alight,
I am told what it says. I cannot distinguish
shaking earth from burnished bridge from

a concise life bound backward. Its characters
evade me. The alcove, the screen, the outcrop,
each refuses my efforts to parse and combine,
each entry a shadow behind thick washi panels.

What I receive is mine, even if it passes the way
a father might demolish his house to build
two smaller houses. It’s enough to see how
a thing is passed on. I don’t need to ask

what it’s good for. What anything
given dutifully could be good for.
